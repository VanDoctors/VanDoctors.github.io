Catching Some Rays

Solar Panels

February 1, 2026

One of the biggest outdoor projects on the van was building a roof rack and installing solar panels. After doing some initial research and learning that OEM roof racks for the Ford Transit can cost upwards of $3,000 we decided to build our own! We started by buying 6 mounting towers from VanConversion. We covered the bottoms of these mounting towers with butyl tape and bolted them to the OEM mounting points on the roof of the van using 2” stainless steel M-8 bolts. Next, we cut and assembled the frame of the roof rack using 1.5” diameter 80/20 aluminum extrusion. Each side of the 80/20 aluminum rods has T shaped channel. T-nuts can slide into these channels and are fastened into position using bolts and washers. This makes installing and moving components on the roof rack easy and flexible, and it opens the door to mounting other components like a storage box or an awning without making major changes to the rack.

After assembling the 80/20 frame on the roof, we started working on the solar panel setup! We bought two 200W rigid Renogy solar panels. We considered using flexible solar panels which stick to the roof and don’t require a roof rack, but decided against them. Flexible solar panels don’t receive airflow on their bottom which reduces cooling, lowers their efficiency and shortens their lifespan and warranty.

We mounted each solar panel on the roof rack using Z-brackets and T-nuts - when we were doing this it was of course the coldest coupled days of the winter and our fingers were freezing.

Around this time, Emilie and Ella visited us in Newburyport! We took a much needed beach walk break on Plum Island, where Emilie and Ella walked away with pockets full of shells. Emile helped with the last few bolts on the solar panel assembly and they both signed the van over the headboard!

After installing the panels, we added 15A fuses on each solar panel to avoid the panels from getting damaged if there’s too much power pulled by the electrical system. Next, we routed the wires through a solar cable gland through the roof of the van. To install the solar cable gland, we drilled a 1” diameter hole into the roof of the van, applied a grommet to the hold, added butyl tape to the bottom of the cable gland and then bolted the gland over the hole. We siliconed the entire perimeter of the gland and each bolt hole and then threaded the solar cable wires through the gland into the ceiling.

The wires run behind the cedar wall paneling down to the electrical cabinet and connect to a 30A breaker. The 30A solar breaker is meant to protect the solar panels in the case of a short or malfunction and to isolate the solar panels from the rest of the electrical system when we’re doing maintenance. The 30A breaker then connects to a Renogy MPPT solar charge controller. The MPPT charge controller converts the 40V input power to 12V output power to charge the battery and has some other fancy software for efficiently charging the battery based on usage. Finally, we connected a bluetooth monitor to the charge controller and installed a Renogy dashboard next the desk. We flipped the whole system on and were successfully generating 140W of power to charge the battery!
