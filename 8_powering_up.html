Powering Up

Electrical System

March 4, 2026

Setting up the electrical system was one of the longest but most enjoyable parts of the van conversion. It started with building a plywood electrical cabinet over the passenger rear-wheel to house all of the components of the electrical system.

We celebrated my birthday, and more importantly successful completion of the electrical cabinet with some New England lobster!

The first component we installed into the electrical cabinets was a 50lb 12V 300Ah Renogy battery which we connected to a kill switch and victron battery monitor. We then installed two ways to charge the battery: 1) shore power and 2) solar panels.

Shore power lets us charge our battery by plugging the van directly into an external power source like a home or campsite outlet. To install shore power we drilled a 1.75” diameter hole into the side of the van and bolted a shore power plug. We connected the plug to a 30A breaker using 10AWG stranded marine grade wire. We then connected the breaker to a 2000W Renogy inverter/charger. The 30A breaker ensures that if we plug the van into a power source that is broken or incorrectly grounded, our main electrical system won’t be damaged. The 2000W inverter charger converts the 120V AC input power to 12V DC output power for charging our 12V battery.

We discuss the solar panel installation in a lot more detail in a separate blog! But as a quick summary, we installed two 200W Renogy solar panels on the roof. The solar panels are connected in parallel to a maximum output power of 400W at roughly 40V. The solar panel cables are routed from the roof through a solar cable gland into the van ceiling, behind the cedar walls, and into the electrical cabinet where they’re connected to a 30A solar breaker. The 30A solar breaker is meant to protect the solar panels in the case of a short or malfunction and to isolate the solar panels from the rest of the electrical system when we’re doing maintenance. The breaker is then connected to a Renogy MPPT charge controller. The MPPT charge controller converts the 40V input power to 12V output power to charge the battery and has some other fancy software for efficiently charging the battery based on usage.

The inverter charger, solar charge controller and battery are all connected to negative and positive bus bars. The bus bars act as centralized terminals for connecting several electrical components. Each terminal on the positive bus bar has space for a fuse to protect that component of the system.

We connect both bus bars to an electrical distribution panel. The electrical panel is where all of the wires that power the outlets, refrigerator, water pump, stove, heater, carbon monoxide detector, and fans originate. The electrical panel is broken into two halves, a 120V half and a 12V half. Each wire on the 12V side is protected using a fuse, chosen based on the gauge of the wire and the amperage of the device it powers. The wire gauge is based on the amperage of the electrical component and the round trip distance an electron must travel from the electrical panel to the appliance it’s powering and back. Each wire on the 120V side is 14AWG and protected using a 15A circuit breaker. We used AFCI breakers for all 120V branch circuits, and all 120V outlets are GCFI. We used wago connectors to connect wire ends and junction boxes to house all wago connectors on the 120V half of the system.

Building the electrical system was an awesome learning experience. We learned how to safely ground an electrical system, use a voltmeter, cut, splice, and crimp wires, wire a 3-way switch, and wire 120V and 12V outlets. The initial tests of the system show that everything is hooked up and working! Though I’m expecting normal bugs will pop up here and there as we stress test and use the system more.
