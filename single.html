Getting started

can a winter workshop be a wonderland

January 5, 2026

We first noticed a couple leaks in the van (shoutout to the frozen water for making it obvious), found the sources with some handy stickers that turn red on contact with water, and blocked the entry points with caulk.

Then comes the question, how to build out a van in the freezing MA winter? We started out by building insulation to mate the van side door to our garage door. Turns out this was a great low-stakes way to learn how to use the jig saw, table saw, circle saw, and even the hole saw (with an excellent use case to block cold air from coming through the wheel). The insulation runs up and down each side of the van and has a hinged roof so we can take the van out if we need to. Now we have a cozy-ish work space where our hands won’t freeze off!

Next we got to start our first full day in the van! We removed the floor panels and did some tedious but satisfying cleaning with degreaser. First blood while working on the van was had for both of us. After setting up a portable propane heater to keep us, and more importantly the van, warm enough, we sanded down any rust we found on the metal and painted with a two-layer rustoleum paint to prevent future rust. We then added sound deadening panels to block vibrational noise.

And we installed one of the most critical comfort aspects for our van: swivel seats! As the supposedly “easiest DIY project” you can do in a van conversion, it required rerouting the electrical connection, cutting through the metal rails of the bottoms of the seat, and some squeezing into tight places.
